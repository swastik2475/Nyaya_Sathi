FIR — First Information Report

What is an FIR?
A First Information Report (FIR) is a written document prepared by the police when they receive information about the commission of a cognisable offence. It is the first step in the criminal justice process. FIR is governed by BNSS Section 173 (formerly CrPC Section 154).

How to File an FIR in India
Step 1: Visit the nearest police station.
Step 2: Give your complaint orally or in writing to the officer-in-charge.
Step 3: The officer must write it down and read it back to you.
Step 4: Sign or give your thumb impression on the FIR.
Step 5: Demand a free copy of the FIR — you are legally entitled to it (BNSS Section 173(2)).

Zero FIR
Under BNSS, you can file a Zero FIR at ANY police station regardless of where the crime occurred. The station that receives the Zero FIR must forward it to the station having jurisdiction.

How to File FIR Online (e-FIR)
Most states now provide online FIR portals:
- National portal: https://www.cybercrime.gov.in (for cybercrime)
- Uttar Pradesh: https://uppolice.gov.in
- Delhi: https://delhipolice.gov.in
- Maharashtra: https://citizen.mahapolice.gov.in
Steps for online FIR:
1. Visit the state police portal or the National Cybercrime Reporting Portal.
2. Click on "File a Complaint" or "Report Cybercrime."
3. Register/login with your mobile number.
4. Fill in the complaint details and upload supporting evidence.
5. Submit — you will receive an acknowledgement number.

What to Do If Police Refuses to Register FIR
If the police officer refuses to register an FIR:
Option 1: Approach the Superintendent of Police (SP) with your written complaint — BNSS Section 173(4).
Option 2: File a complaint before the Executive Magistrate.
Option 3: File a private complaint under Section 223 BNSS directly before a Magistrate.
Option 4: Approach the High Court under Article 226 for a writ of mandamus directing the police to register the FIR.

The Supreme Court in Lalita Kumari v. Govt. of U.P. (2014) held that registration of FIR is mandatory when the information discloses a cognisable offence — there is no discretion to conduct a preliminary inquiry first.

Cognisable vs. Non-Cognisable Offences
- Cognisable offences (e.g. murder, rape, robbery, theft): Police can arrest without a warrant and must register FIR.
- Non-cognisable offences (e.g. assault, cheating of small amounts): Police cannot arrest without a warrant; a complaint is filed and a warrant is obtained.

Emergency: Dial 112 for Police emergency. Dial 1930 for cybercrime helpline.
